Unit Plan: Notation And Musical Time

Back to Grade 6Open Blackhole

Description

Students investigate how modern music notation communicates musical time: pulse, bars, time signatures, note values, and rests. History is used only as a short starter to show why notation was invented. Students then apply this understanding practically by performing and creating short rhythms from notation, and finish by reflecting on how reading and writing rhythm changed their musicianship. This is the first unit of MYP music and deliberately builds the rhythm-literacy foundation every later unit depends on.

Inquiry Questions

Factual

  • What is a pulse, a bar, a time signature, a note value, and a rest?
  • How does notation show when sound happens and when silence happens?
  • How do musicians count time when they play?

Conceptual

  • How does a written system communicate something we can only hear?
  • Why do musicians need a shared system to play together?
  • What makes a notation system clear or unclear?

Debatable

  • Could musicians play together without any notation at all?
  • Is notation the best way to communicate rhythm, or just the most common one?

Curriculum

Aims

  • Understand the relationship between art and its contexts
  • Develop the skills necessary to create and to perform art
  • Communicate through artistic expression

MYP Subject Group Objectives

  • Objective A: Investigating (Year 1 / Novice)
  • i: Investigate a musical context related to the statement of inquiry.
  • ii: Describe an artwork or performance from the chosen context.
  • Objective B: Developing
  • Practically explore rhythmic ideas from notation to inform a final performance/creation.
  • Present a clear intention for the final rhythm work in line with the statement of inquiry.
  • Objective C: Creating/Performing
  • Perform and/or create a short rhythm piece from notation.
  • Objective D: Evaluating
  • Appraise their own rhythm work.
  • Reflect on their development as a musician.

Content

Factual Knowledge

  • Pulse/beat, bars and barlines, time signatures (2/4, 3/4, 4/4)
  • Note values: whole, half, quarter, eighth; corresponding rests
  • Counting systems (1-2-3-4, "1 and 2 and")
  • Why notation exists: memory, sharing, coordination (starter only)

Conceptual Understanding

  • Notation is a communication system: symbols represent sound and silence in time
  • Shared structure (bars, metre) is what lets musicians coordinate
  • A rhythm can be read, written, and reproduced by someone who never heard it

Procedural Knowledge

  • Read and count a short rhythm from notation
  • Clap/play a notated rhythm accurately against a steady pulse
  • Annotate a music sheet: label time signature, barlines, note values, rests
  • Write a short original rhythm in correct notation

Skills

Subject-Specific Skills

  • Steady-pulse keeping and counting
  • Rhythm reading and clapping/playing
  • Accurate annotation with correct music vocabulary
  • Basic rhythm writing/composition

ATL Skills

  • Communication: explain time notation in an infographic using correct vocabulary; annotate an example so someone else can understand it
  • Organization (Self-Management): plan the A3 infographic layout; meet staged checkpoints before submission
  • Critical Thinking: identify what a notation example does and does not show about time

IB Learner Profile

Attribute: Communicators Students develop the Communicator attribute by learning to express musical time through a shared symbolic system. They explain notation concepts visually in an infographic, annotate real music so others can follow their thinking, and experience how precise shared language (bars, values, rests) allows people to make music together.

International Mindedness

Music notation is one of the most widely shared communication systems in the world: a rhythm written in one country can be played accurately in another with no common spoken language. Students briefly encounter the historical need for notation and consider how a shared system crosses cultures, while acknowledging that many musical traditions transmit rhythm orally rather than in writing.

Connections

Language Development

The unit builds a small, precise vocabulary (pulse, bar, barline, time signature, note value, rest, annotate), each defined on first use in student materials. Sentence starters and a symbol bank support struggling-English students; the infographic format lets students show understanding visually as well as verbally.

Information Communication Technology

The main products are deliberately paper-based (A3 infographic, hand annotation) to keep the focus on notation itself. Students may optionally annotate digitally. Later in the unit, notated rhythms are entered into BandLab as an introduction to digital sequencing, connecting written notation to grid/piano-roll representation.

Learning Experiences

In teaching order:

  1. Starter story: why notation was invented — music had to be remembered, shared, and coordinated (history used only here).
  2. Pulse and beat: clap-alongs, counting games, keeping steady time as a class.
  3. Bars, barlines, and time signatures: reading and counting 2/4, 3/4, 4/4 with count-along checks.
  4. Note values and rests: rhythm reading, clap-back checks, sound vs. silence.
  5. Guided annotation practice on a partially annotated rhythm sheet (whole class, then pairs).
  6. Infographic planning: layout options, draft checkpoint, teacher conference.
  7. Students choose (or get approved) a music sheet and annotate it; peer check "can you clap my rhythm?".
  8. Final infographic and annotation completed and submitted; BandLab notation-to-grid intro follows as the bridge to practical work.

Assessment

Summative Tasks

  • Task A (Criterion A): A3 infographic on time notation + annotated music sheet — see 6_TaskA_Notation_Time.
  • Task BC (Criteria B, C): to be planned.
  • Task D (Criterion D): written reflection appraising their own work and their development as a musician.

Formative Assessment

  • Clap-back and count-along checks during lessons
  • Draft infographic checkpoint with teacher conference
  • Partially annotated rhythm sheet as guided practice before the assessed annotation
  • Peer check: "Can you clap my rhythm from my notation?"

Peer and Self-assessment

Students use the student rubric in the Task A document to self-check and give peer feedback before submission ("Can someone understand my infographic without me explaining it?").

Standardization and Moderation

As the sole music teacher, standardization relies on consistent application of MYP Arts Year 1 criteria, the task-specific clarification in the Task A document, and holistic review of marks across classes.

Feedback

Feedback is continuous and verbal during rhythm practice, plus written/conference feedback at the infographic draft checkpoint. Criterion-referenced comments point students to the specific rubric strand they can improve before final submission.

Differentiation

Advanced Students

  • Compare 2/4, 3/4, and 4/4; include ties and varied rest values
  • Create and annotate an original rhythm
  • Explain why time signatures and barlines make coordination possible

Struggling Students

  • Time-symbol bank and partially annotated rhythm sheet
  • Infographic layout options and staged checkpoints
  • Shorter rhythm examples with fewer note values

ELL Support

  • Vocabulary list with plain-language definitions and symbols
  • Sentence starters for annotations
  • Visual-heavy infographic accepted; teacher conference during planning

Resources

  • A3 paper, colours, drawing materials
  • Short rhythm sheets at varied difficulty (teacher-curated, printable)
  • Time-symbol bank and partially annotated example (The Pantry)
  • Body percussion / classroom percussion instruments
  • BandLab (free online DAW) for the notation-to-grid connection
  • Short starter video/story on why notation was invented
