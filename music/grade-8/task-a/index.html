Task A: Drum And Percussion Notation

Back to Grade 8Open Blackhole

Submission Tag: taskA

What This Task Is About

Drum and percussion parts are mostly about rhythm. But without a clear way to write them down, it is hard to share, remember, or play together.

In this task, you will investigate how drum and percussion notation helps musicians communicate rhythm, groove, layers, and genre style.

Statement of inquiry: Drum notation lets musicians communicate rhythm, groove, and genre style so a beat can be shared and reproduced.

What You Must Do

You must complete both parts.

  1. Answer all guided questions from The Pantry.
  2. Annotate one drumming or percussion music sheet.

This is because Criterion A has two strands:

  • Strand i: investigate the context of drum/percussion notation.
  • Strand ii: analyse one notation example.

Part 1: Guided Questions

Answer every guided question in The Pantry.

You may write your answers anywhere you are comfortable:

  • Google Docs
  • Notepad
  • another writing app

When you are finished, export or save your answers as a Markdown file: .md

Then upload the .md file to the Blackhole.

Important: if you use Notepad on a school PC, your work is local. That means it may only be saved on that computer. You may not be able to work on it from home. Someone could also delete it. Save carefully and ask for help if needed.

Your answers should explain how drum and percussion notation communicates rhythm and groove.

You may discuss things like:

  • pulse and metre = the steady count and how it is grouped
  • beat and groove = the repeating feel of the rhythm
  • repeated patterns = the same rhythm played more than once
  • layers and instrumental roles = which drum or part plays what
  • drum kit notation = how a full drum set is written down
  • percussion notation symbols = symbols for cymbals, blocks, shakers, etc.
  • genre style = how the notation changes for rock, funk, pop, world music
  • basic and advanced beats = what makes a beat simple or complex
  • why notation helps performers reproduce a rhythm = so they can play it the same way more than once

Write in full sentences. Use details from the videos. Explain your ideas clearly, like you are explaining them to a younger sibling.

Part 2: Annotated Drumming / Percussion Music Sheet

Choose one teacher-approved drumming or percussion music sheet.

Annotate means: add notes or labels that explain what you can see.

Your annotation should identify:

  • metre / time signature
  • repeated rhythmic pattern
  • instruments or percussion sounds
  • layers or roles
  • accents
  • rests
  • syncopation, if present
  • genre features
  • what makes the beat basic or advanced
  • how the notation helps a performer reproduce the groove

Try to explain all visible parts or symbols you can identify. Do not only label one or two things.

You may annotate in different ways:

  • draw or type on the image
  • use Google Draw
  • use Excalidraw
  • print it and annotate by hand

The tool does not matter. Clear explanation matters.

If you annotate by hand, give it to me. I will scan and upload it for you.

Support And Extension

Everyone does the same task. You can use these supports:

  • the drum notation key from The Pantry
  • sentence starters for the guided questions
  • the example annotation as a model
  • teacher-suggested music sheets with clear, repeated patterns

Want a challenge?

  • compare a basic and an advanced beat in the same genre
  • explain how one genre's groove shows up in the notation

How This Is Marked (Criterion A: Investigating)

This task is marked out of 8.

  • 7-8 — Sophisticated: You answer all questions with strong detail. Your annotation explains the notation clearly and connects it to how rhythm, groove, layers, and genre style are communicated.
  • 5-6 — Proficient: You answer all parts correctly with some detail. Your annotation explains the main symbols and how the notation shows beat, layers, and groove.
  • 3-4 — Developing: You complete most of the task, but some answers or annotations are too short, unclear, or missing detail.
  • 1-2 — Beginning: Very little is complete. The answers or annotation are hard to understand.
  • 0 — Not submitted.
