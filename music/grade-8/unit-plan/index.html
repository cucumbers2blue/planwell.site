Unit Plan: Drum And Percussion Notation

Back to Grade 8Open Blackhole

Description

Students investigate how drum and percussion notation communicates rhythm, groove, layers, and genre style. They study pulse and metre, repeated patterns, drum kit and percussion symbols, instrumental roles, and what separates a basic beat from an advanced one across genres such as rock, funk, pop, and world music. They then analyse one drumming or percussion music sheet in detail, explaining how the notation lets a performer reproduce the groove. The unit deepens the rhythm literacy built in earlier grades and points toward practical beat-making work.

Inquiry Questions

Factual

  • How is a full drum kit written down on one staff?
  • What symbols are used for cymbals, blocks, shakers, and other percussion?
  • What are accents, rests, and syncopation in a drum part?

Conceptual

  • How does notation capture something as physical as a groove?
  • How do layers and instrumental roles combine into one rhythm?
  • What makes a beat basic or advanced?

Debatable

  • Can a written drum part ever fully capture the feel of a live drummer?
  • Do drummers in different genres need notation, or is listening enough?

Curriculum

Aims

  • Understand the relationship between art and its contexts
  • Develop the skills necessary to create and to perform art
  • Communicate through artistic expression

MYP Subject Group Objectives

  • Objective A: Investigating (Year 3 / Intermediate)
  • i: Investigate the context of drum and percussion notation, related to the statement of inquiry.
  • ii: Analyse one drumming or percussion notation example.
  • Objective B: Developing
  • Practically explore ideas to inform development of a final artwork or performance.
  • Present a clear artistic intention for the final artwork or performance in line with the statement of inquiry.
  • Objective C: Creating/Performing
  • Create or perform an artwork.
  • Objective D: Evaluating
  • Appraise their own artwork or performance.
  • Reflect on their development as a musician.

Content

Factual Knowledge

  • Pulse, metre, and time signatures in drum parts
  • Drum kit notation: which line or space is which drum or cymbal
  • Percussion notation symbols for auxiliary instruments
  • Repeated patterns, accents, rests, and syncopation
  • Genre conventions: rock, funk, pop, and world music beats

Conceptual Understanding

  • Drum notation is a communication system for groove, not just rhythm
  • A beat is built from layers with distinct instrumental roles
  • Genre style is visible in notation: pattern, layering, and accent choices
  • Notation is what lets a groove be reproduced the same way twice

Procedural Knowledge

  • Read a drum kit part using a notation key
  • Identify metre, repeated patterns, layers, accents, rests, and syncopation in a score
  • Compare basic and advanced beats and justify the difference
  • Annotate a music sheet with labels and short explanations

Skills

Subject-Specific Skills

  • Drum and percussion score reading
  • Groove analysis: layers, roles, repetition, syncopation
  • Genre identification from notation features
  • Accurate annotation with correct rhythm vocabulary

ATL Skills

  • Communication: annotate a drum sheet so another drummer could follow the reasoning
  • Critical Thinking: judge what makes a beat basic or advanced and support the judgment with evidence
  • Research (Information Literacy): take structured notes from videos; choose a suitable music sheet

IB Learner Profile

Attribute: Thinkers Students develop the Thinker attribute by deconstructing beats analytically: separating layers, identifying roles, tracing repetition and variation, and reasoning about why a groove works. The annotation task requires evidence-based judgment, not just labeling.

International Mindedness

Groove traditions come from everywhere: rock and funk from American traditions, and percussion patterns from West African, Latin American, and Asian musical cultures. Students see that notation adapts to each style, and that many of the world's most influential rhythms began in cultures that never needed to write them down.

Connections

Language Development

The unit builds precise rhythm vocabulary (metre, groove, layer, accent, syncopation), defined in plain language on first use. Guided questions scaffold full-sentence writing; annotation lets students demonstrate understanding visually with short labels.

Information Communication Technology

Students take notes from teacher-selected videos, use a digital drum notation key, annotate digitally with Google Draw or Excalidraw (or by hand, scanned by the teacher), export writing as Markdown, and practise safe file storage habits.

Learning Experiences

In teaching order:

  1. Groove listening starter: play one famous beat, pull it apart by ear — how many layers, who does what?
  2. Drum kit notation key lessons: which line or space is which drum/cymbal; tap-along reading of simple kit parts.
  3. Teacher-selected videos with guided note-taking; work sessions on the guided questions.
  4. Genre comparison listening: the same drum kit, different grooves — rock, funk, pop, world music — and how each looks in notation.
  5. Study the example annotation; class discussion on what makes a beat basic or advanced.
  6. Music sheet choice and approval conferences; drum notation key kept at hand.
  7. Draft annotation with verbal checks: "which layer is the backbone here?".
  8. Finalize: export answers as .md, complete the annotation, upload to the Blackhole.

Assessment

Summative Tasks

  • Task A (Criterion A): guided question answers (.md) + annotated drum/percussion music sheet (.jpg) — see 8_TaskA_Drum_Percussion_Notation.
  • Task BC (Criteria B, C): to be planned.
  • Task D (Criterion D): written reflection appraising their own work and their development as a musician.

Formative Assessment

  • Clap/tap-along groove checks during lessons
  • Music sheet choice approved by the teacher before final annotation begins
  • Draft annotation compared against the example annotation in The Pantry
  • Short verbal checks: "Which layer is the backbone here? What makes this beat advanced?"

Peer and Self-assessment

Students use the "Before You Submit" checklist in the Task A document and compare their draft annotation against the model example before submission.

Standardization and Moderation

As the sole music teacher, standardization relies on consistent application of MYP Arts Year 3 criteria, the marking bands in the Task A document, and holistic review of marks across classes.

Feedback

Feedback is continuous and verbal during listening and reading lessons, with a focused conference at music sheet approval. Criterion-referenced comments on draft annotations target the common gap — describing symbols without explaining groove, layers, or genre — before final submission.

Differentiation

Advanced Students

  • Choose a sheet with syncopation, fills, or complex layering
  • Compare a basic and an advanced beat in the same genre
  • Explain how one genre's groove conventions appear in notation

Struggling Students

  • Teacher-suggested music sheets with clear, repetitive patterns
  • Drum notation key kept at hand throughout
  • Guided questions answered with sentence starters

ELL Support

  • Vocabulary list with plain-language definitions
  • Answers accepted in short, simple sentences
  • Annotation task allows visual demonstration of understanding

Resources

  • Drum and percussion music sheets at varied difficulty
  • Google Draw / Excalidraw for digital annotation; printing and scanning for paper annotation
