Unit Plan: The History Of Music Notation

Back to Grade 7Open Blackhole

Description

Students investigate how music notation evolved: from oral tradition through neumes and early chant notation to staff notation and printed music. They study why each change happened — pitch accuracy, rhythm, ensemble coordination, teaching, sharing — and analyse one historical manuscript in detail, identifying what its notation communicates and what still depended on performer knowledge. The unit shows notation as a living communication system that changed whenever musicians needed it to work better.

Inquiry Questions

Factual

  • What did early music notation look like, and what could it show?
  • What is oral tradition, and how was music shared before notation?
  • When and why did staff notation and printed music appear?

Conceptual

  • Why do communication systems change over time?
  • What musical needs drove each change in notation?
  • How much of a notation system depends on what the reader already knows?

Debatable

  • Was the invention of notation entirely a good thing for music?
  • Could a modern musician perform accurately from a medieval manuscript?

Curriculum

Aims

  • Understand the relationship between art and its contexts
  • Develop the skills necessary to create and to perform art
  • Communicate through artistic expression

MYP Subject Group Objectives

  • Objective A: Investigating (Year 3 / Intermediate)
  • i: Investigate the historical context of music notation, related to the statement of inquiry.
  • ii: Analyse one historical manuscript or notation example.
  • Objective B: Developing
  • Practically explore ideas to inform development of a final artwork or performance.
  • Present a clear artistic intention for the final artwork or performance in line with the statement of inquiry.
  • Objective C: Creating/Performing
  • Create or perform an artwork.
  • Objective D: Evaluating
  • Appraise their own artwork or performance.
  • Reflect on their development as a musician.

Content

Factual Knowledge

  • Oral tradition and why music needed to be written down
  • Neumes, chant notation, and church music
  • The development of the staff, pitch accuracy, and rhythm notation
  • Music printing and the sharing of music
  • Notation as a teaching tool

Conceptual Understanding

  • Notation systems evolve in response to real musical needs
  • Every notation system communicates some things clearly and leaves others to the performer
  • A manuscript is evidence of what musicians of its time needed and knew

Procedural Knowledge

  • Extract and organize information from teacher-selected videos
  • Choose and evaluate a suitable historical manuscript
  • Annotate a manuscript: identify symbols, what they communicate, and what they leave unclear
  • Write clear explanations in full sentences and export as Markdown

Skills

Subject-Specific Skills

  • Reading and interpreting historical notation examples
  • Connecting notation features to musical needs of a period
  • Accurate annotation with correct music vocabulary
  • Explaining the difference between written and orally transmitted music

ATL Skills

  • Research (Information Literacy): find a suitable historical manuscript; take structured notes from videos
  • Communication: annotate an image so someone else can understand each symbol; explain ideas in full sentences
  • Critical Thinking: judge what a notation system communicates clearly and what it does not

IB Learner Profile

Attribute: Inquirers Students develop the Inquirer attribute by investigating an unfamiliar historical world through its artifacts. They ask what each strange symbol meant, why it existed, and what problem it solved, and they follow those questions through guided research and their own chosen manuscript.

International Mindedness

Notation history crosses cultures and centuries: chant manuscripts, early European staff notation, and oral traditions from many parts of the world all solve the same problem differently. Students consider that writing music down is one solution among several, and that many musical cultures transmit sophisticated music without notation at all.

Connections

Language Development

The unit introduces historical and technical vocabulary (oral tradition, manuscript, neume, staff, chant), each defined in plain language on first use. Guided questions scaffold written answers; students write in full sentences at their own level, and annotation lets them show understanding visually.

Information Communication Technology

Students research using teacher-selected videos and online manuscript sources, practise saving work safely (local vs. cloud storage), export writing as Markdown, and annotate digitally with tools like Google Draw or Excalidraw — or by hand, scanned by the teacher.

Learning Experiences

In teaching order:

  1. Starter: teach the class a short rhythm by ear only, then by notation — what was easier, and why did people need to write music down?
  2. Teacher-selected videos on notation history with guided note-taking (oral tradition, chant, neumes, staff, printing).
  3. Work sessions on the guided questions from The Pantry, with sentence starters for those who need them.
  4. Study the example annotation together: what makes an annotation explain rather than just label.
  5. Manuscript hunt: students find a historical manuscript; approval conferences before final annotation begins.
  6. Draft annotation compared against the model; verbal checks on "what does this symbol tell the performer — and what doesn't it?".
  7. Finalize: export answers as .md, complete the annotation, upload to the Blackhole.

Assessment

Summative Tasks

  • Task A (Criterion A): guided question answers (.md) + annotated historical manuscript (.jpg) — see 7_TaskA_Notation_History.
  • Task BC (Criteria B, C): to be planned.
  • Task D (Criterion D): written reflection appraising their own work and their development as a musician.

Formative Assessment

  • Video note-taking checks during lessons
  • Manuscript choice approved by the teacher before final annotation begins
  • Draft annotation compared against the example annotation in The Pantry
  • Short verbal checks: "What does this symbol tell the performer? What doesn't it tell them?"

Peer and Self-assessment

Students use the "Before You Submit" checklist in the Task A document and compare their draft annotation against the model example before submission.

Standardization and Moderation

As the sole music teacher, standardization relies on consistent application of MYP Arts Year 3 criteria, the marking bands in the Task A document, and holistic review of marks across classes.

Feedback

Feedback is continuous and verbal during research lessons, with a focused conference at manuscript approval. Criterion-referenced comments on draft annotations point students to specific gaps — unexplained symbols, missing "what it doesn't show" analysis — before final submission.

Differentiation

Advanced Students

  • Choose a more complex or unusual manuscript
  • Compare two manuscripts from different periods
  • Explain how a specific notation change solved a specific musical problem

Struggling Students

  • Teacher-suggested manuscript options with clearer symbols
  • Guided questions answered with sentence starters
  • Example annotation used as a close model

ELL Support

  • Vocabulary list with plain-language definitions
  • Answers accepted in short, simple sentences
  • Annotation task allows visual demonstration of understanding

Resources
