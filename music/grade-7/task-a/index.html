Task A: History Of Music Notation

Back to Grade 7Open Blackhole

Submission Tag: taskA

What This Task Is About

Music notation did not always look like it does today.

In this task, you will investigate how notation changed over time.

Old notation helps us understand why modern notation exists. It also shows how communication systems change when people need them to work better.

Statement of inquiry: Notation systems change over time as musicians' needs to communicate change.

What You Must Do

You must complete both parts.

  1. Answer all guided questions from The Pantry.
  2. Annotate one historical manuscript or old notation example.

This is because Criterion A has two strands:

  • Strand i: investigate the historical context.
  • Strand ii: analyse one example.

Where To Find Things

  • The guided questions are in The Pantry.
  • The task instructions are in The Pantry.
  • The Blackhole upload link is in The Pantry and/or this task page.
  • The videos will be given by your teacher (it'll be here)
  • The example annotation is in The Pantry.
  • You must find your own historical manuscript or notation example.

Before you start your final annotation, show me your manuscript. If you are not sure it is suitable, ask.

Part 1: Guided Questions

Answer every guided question in The Pantry.

You may write your answers anywhere you are comfortable:

  • Google Docs
  • Notepad
  • another writing app

When you are finished, export or save your answers as a Markdown file: .md

Then upload the .md file to the Blackhole.

Important: if you use Notepad on a school PC, your work is local. That means it may only be saved on that computer. You may not be able to work on it from home. Someone could also delete it. Save carefully and ask for help if needed.

Your answers should explain how musical needs changed over time.

You may discuss things like:

  • oral tradition = people told each other how to sing or play, instead of writing it down
  • pitch accuracy
  • rhythm and timing
  • ensemble coordination
  • church music and chant
  • staff notation
  • printing and sharing music
  • notation as a teaching tool

Write in full sentences. Use details from the videos. Explain your ideas clearly, like you are explaining them to a younger sibling.

Part 2: Annotated Historical Manuscript

Choose one historical manuscript or old notation example.

Annotate means: add notes or labels that explain what you can see.

Your annotation should explain:

  • what symbols or marks you notice
  • what parts show pitch, rhythm, text, gesture, structure, or performance instructions
  • what the notation communicates clearly
  • what the notation does not explain clearly
  • what the performer probably needed to already know
  • how this example shows a musical need from that time

Try to explain all visible parts or symbols you can identify. Do not only label one or two things.

You may annotate in different ways:

  • draw or type on the image
  • use Google Draw
  • use Excalidraw
  • print it and annotate by hand

The tool does not matter. Clear explanation matters.

If you annotate by hand, give it to me. I will scan and upload it for you.

Support And Extension

Everyone does the same task. You can use these supports:

  • sentence starters for the guided questions
  • the example annotation as a model
  • teacher-suggested manuscripts with clearer symbols
  • a teacher check before your final annotation

Want a challenge?

  • choose a more complex or unusual manuscript
  • compare two manuscripts from different periods
  • explain how one notation change solved one musical problem

How This Is Marked (Criterion A: Investigating)

This task is marked out of 8.

  • 7-8 — Sophisticated: You answer all questions with strong detail. Your annotation explains the manuscript clearly and connects it to the history of notation.
  • 5-6 — Proficient: You answer all parts correctly with some detail. Your annotation explains the main symbols and what the notation communicates.
  • 3-4 — Developing: You complete most of the task, but some answers or annotations are too short, unclear, or missing detail.
  • 1-2 — Beginning: Very little is complete. The answers or annotation are hard to understand.
  • 0 — Not submitted.
