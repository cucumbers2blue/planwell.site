Unit Plan: Jazz And Blues

Back to Grade 9Open Blackhole

Description

Students investigate jazz and blues as genres built on a paradox: the same repeating structures — 12-bar blues, chord progressions, riffs — produce endlessly different performances. They research the historical and cultural roots of blues and jazz in African American musical traditions, study the key musical features (blues form, I-IV-V, blue notes, swing, call-and-response, improvisation), and critique one approved performance or composition in depth. The unit prepares students for later practical work performing or creating their own 12-bar blues, riffs, walking bass, and improvisation.

Inquiry Questions

Factual

  • What is the 12-bar blues form and the I-IV-V progression?
  • Where did blues and jazz come from, and how are they connected?
  • What are riffs, walking bass, blue notes, swing, and call-and-response?

Conceptual

  • How can a fixed structure create freedom rather than limit it?
  • How does music communicate personal and cultural identity?
  • Why does the same form sound different in every performance?

Debatable

  • Is improvisation more expressive than composed music?
  • Does jazz/blues still carry its original cultural meaning when performed today, anywhere in the world?

Curriculum

Aims

  • Understand the relationship between art and its contexts
  • Develop the skills necessary to create and to perform art
  • Communicate through artistic expression

MYP Subject Group Objectives

  • Objective A: Investigating (Year 5 / Competent)
  • i: Investigate a movement or genre in music, related to the statement of inquiry.
  • ii: Critique an artwork or performance from the chosen movement or genre.
  • Objective B: Developing
  • Practically explore ideas to inform development of a final artwork or performance.
  • Present a clear artistic intention for the final artwork or performance in line with the statement of inquiry.
  • Objective C: Creating/Performing
  • Create or perform an artwork.
  • Objective D: Evaluating
  • Appraise their own artwork or performance.
  • Reflect on their development as a musician.

Content

Factual Knowledge

  • Origins of blues: work songs, spirituals, field hollers, call-and-response
  • Early blues and jazz development, migration, and performance venues
  • 12-bar blues form, I-IV-V progression, dominant seventh chords
  • Blues scale, blue notes, riffs, walking bass, swing feel
  • Key artists and traditions connected to students' chosen examples

Conceptual Understanding

  • Repetition provides the stable framework; expression and improvisation personalize it
  • Musical features carry identity: who plays, how, and where shapes meaning
  • A genre is a living tradition, not a fixed set of rules
  • Critique requires evidence: timestamps, structure maps, and musical vocabulary

Procedural Knowledge

  • Research history, artist, and recording using credible sources
  • Map a chord progression and locate the 12-bar structure by ear
  • Deconstruct a recording with timestamps, listening timelines, and annotated moments
  • Build a presentation package: slides, full presenter notes, and a source list
  • Present live and answer follow-up questions

Skills

Subject-Specific Skills

  • Aural analysis of form, harmony, riffs, and improvisation
  • Genre-accurate vocabulary (blue notes, swing, call-and-response, walking bass)
  • Evidence-based critique of a specific recording
  • Connecting musical features to historical and cultural context

ATL Skills

  • Research (Information Literacy): evaluate and cite at least four credible sources across history, artist, recording, and musical evidence
  • Communication: build slides that support a spoken presentation; write full presenter notes
  • Critical Thinking: move from describing what happens to explaining how the music creates meaning

IB Learner Profile

Attribute: Open-minded Students develop the Open-minded attribute by engaging seriously with the African American cultural traditions that produced blues and jazz, evaluating the music on its own terms and its own history, and considering how meaning travels — and changes — when the music is performed by others.

International Mindedness

Blues and jazz grew from African American experience and became global musical languages. Students examine how migration, social context, and cultural exchange shaped the music, and how a local tradition can become a worldwide form of expression while raising real questions about origin, credit, and meaning.

Connections

Language Development

The unit requires sustained academic writing in presenter notes and precise genre vocabulary, supported by a vocabulary bank and sentence starters. The live presentation develops spoken academic language; follow-up questions check genuine understanding beneath the prepared text.

Information Communication Technology

Students research online with source evaluation, reference specific recordings with timestamps, build slide decks, capture annotated screenshots, and submit a multi-file package (slides, Markdown or PDF notes) to the Blackhole.

Learning Experiences

In teaching order:

  1. Shared listening: one classic 12-bar blues; locate the form and the I-IV-V changes by ear as a class, using the chord map.
  2. History lessons: origins of blues — work songs, spirituals, field hollers, call-and-response — and the migration into jazz.
  3. Musical features lessons: blues scale and blue notes, riffs, walking bass, swing feel, improvisation; short guided listening for each.
  4. Study the model presentation: the difference between describing and critiquing, using the weak/stronger example pairs.
  5. Example choice and approval conferences; planning conference on each student's focus question.
  6. Research and guided listening sessions; draft deconstruction of one short excerpt checked against the model.
  7. Build slides and presenter notes; pair rehearsal with structured feedback.
  8. Live presentations with teacher follow-up questions.

Assessment

Summative Tasks

  • Task A (Criterion A): presentation package — slides, mandatory presenter notes, source list — critiquing one approved jazz/blues example, presented live — see 9_TaskA_Jazz_Blues.
  • Task BC (Criteria B, C): to be planned.
  • Task D (Criterion D): written reflection appraising their own work and their development as a musician.

Formative Assessment

  • Example choice approved by the teacher before analysis begins
  • Guided listening sessions locating the 12-bar form and features by ear
  • Draft deconstruction of one short excerpt against the model presentation
  • Planning conference on focus question and section coverage

Peer and Self-assessment

Students check their package against the nine required sections and the submission checklist, and rehearse presentations in pairs with structured feedback before presenting to the class.

Standardization and Moderation

As the sole music teacher, standardization relies on consistent application of MYP Arts Year 5 criteria, the marking bands in the Task A document, and holistic review of marks across classes; follow-up questioning verifies authenticity.

Feedback

Feedback is given at example approval, during guided listening, and at the draft-deconstruction checkpoint. Criterion-referenced comments target the key Year 5 gap — description instead of critique — pushing students toward evidence, mechanism, and meaning.

Differentiation

Advanced Students

  • Compare two performances of the same standard
  • Analyse a complex improvisation or chord substitutions
  • Transcribe a short riff or solo phrase

Struggling Students

  • Teacher-approved example list with clearer structures
  • 12-bar chord map and listening timeline templates
  • Model annotation of one short excerpt as a close guide

ELL Support

  • Jazz/blues vocabulary bank with plain-language definitions
  • Sentence starters for analysis; visual-heavy slides accepted
  • Presenter notes may use short, simple sentences

Resources

  • Model presentation, vocabulary bank, and 12-bar blues chord map (The Pantry)
  • Approved recordings and example list
  • Listening timeline and analysis templates
  • Credible sources: books, museum/education sites, interviews, documentaries, reputable analysis videos
