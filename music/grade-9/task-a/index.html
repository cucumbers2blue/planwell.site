Task A: Jazz And Blues Investigation

Back to Grade 9Open Blackhole

Submission Tag: taskA

What This Task Is About

Jazz and blues musicians use the same repeating structures — the 12-bar blues, chord progressions, riffs — but every performance sounds different. That is the point: repetition gives a stable framework; expression and improvisation make it personal.

In this task, you will investigate how jazz and blues work, then deconstruct one specific performance or composition in detail.

This will prepare you for later practical work where you will play or create your own 12-bar blues, riffs, walking bass, call-and-response, or improvisation.

What You Must Do

You must complete both parts.

  1. Investigate the context — research jazz/blues history, musical features, and your chosen artist.
  2. Critique one specific performance or composition — deconstruct it in detail with evidence and analysis.

This is because Criterion A has two strands:

  • Strand i: investigate a movement or genre in music.
  • Strand ii: critique an artwork or performance from that genre.

Where To Find Things

  • The model presentation example is in The Pantry.
  • The Blackhole upload link is in The Pantry and/or this task page.
  • A vocabulary bank for jazz/blues terms is in The Pantry.
  • A 12-bar blues chord map is in The Pantry.

You may propose your own composition or performance, but it must be approved by me before you start working on it.

Your example must:

  • clearly connect to jazz and/or blues
  • have enough reliable information for research
  • contain clear musical features for analysis
  • have a specific recording or version you can reference
  • be school-appropriate
  • be complex enough for Grade 9, but not so difficult that it becomes unmanageable

Possible example types:

  • a classic 12-bar blues performance
  • a jazz blues standard
  • a blues-influenced jazz composition
  • a vocal blues performance
  • an instrumental jazz performance that uses blues form or blues vocabulary

Your Presentation Package

You will submit one presentation package containing:

  1. Presentation slides — help the audience see and hear your ideas
  2. Mandatory presenter notes — your full explanation, not only keywords
  3. Source list / bibliography

You will also present your work in class. I may ask follow-up questions to check your understanding.

Important: presenter notes are mandatory written evidence. The slides can be visual; the presenter notes show the depth of your investigation and analysis.

Required Presentation Sections

You do not need to follow these as exact slide numbers, but every section must appear somewhere in your presentation.

1. Your Approved Example And Focus

Include:

  • title of the composition/performance
  • artist/composer/performer
  • year or approximate period
  • exact recording or version you are analysing
  • your main investigation question

Example focus questions:

  • How does this performance use 12-bar blues form as a framework for expression?
  • How does this musician use riffs, call-and-response, and improvisation?
  • How does the performance connect blues history to jazz performance practice?

2. Historical And Cultural Context

Investigate the background of jazz/blues.

You may discuss:

  • origins of blues
  • African American musical traditions
  • work songs, spirituals, field hollers, or call-and-response
  • early blues and jazz development
  • migration, social context, or performance venues
  • how blues influenced jazz and later popular music

You do not need to write the full history of jazz and blues. Choose context that helps explain your specific example.

3. Artist / Composer / Performance Background

Investigate the musician(s) connected to your example.

Include:

  • who created or performed the music
  • why they are significant
  • what style or tradition they represent
  • how their background connects to the music
  • what sources say about their musical approach

4. Key Musical Features Of Jazz / Blues

Explain the musical features that matter for your example.

Possible features:

  • 12-bar blues form
  • I-IV-V chord progression
  • dominant seventh chords
  • blues scale / blue notes
  • riffs
  • walking bass
  • swing feel
  • call-and-response
  • improvisation
  • vocal expression: bends, slides, growls, or instrumental imitation of voice

Use music vocabulary accurately. Define terms when needed.

5. Detailed Deconstruction Of Your Example

Analyse the recording or performance closely.

Include specific evidence such as:

  • timestamps
  • annotated screenshots
  • chord progression map
  • lyric excerpt, if relevant
  • short notation excerpt, if available
  • listening timeline
  • description of what happens in each section

Your analysis should explain how the music works, not only what happens.

Prompts:

  • Where do you hear the 12-bar structure or repeated harmonic pattern?
  • What riffs or repeated ideas appear?
  • How does the bass support the groove?
  • Where does call-and-response happen?
  • Where does improvisation appear?
  • How does the performer create expression?
  • How do rhythm, harmony, melody, timbre, and texture shape the performance?

6. How The Music Communicates Meaning

Critique how the music communicates identity, emotion, style, or cultural meaning.

Go beyond description.

Weak: "The song is blues because it sounds sad." Stronger: "The repeated 12-bar structure gives the performer a stable framework, while the bent vocal notes and improvised guitar responses create a feeling of personal expression."

Consider:

  • What does the music communicate?
  • Which musical choices create that meaning?
  • How does the performance reflect its historical or cultural context?
  • What makes the performance effective or significant?
  • What would be lost if the blues/jazz features were removed?

7. Connection To The Statement Of Inquiry

Answer directly:

How does this example show that musicians use repeated harmonic structures to communicate identity, expression, and improvisational freedom?

Use evidence from your research and your musical analysis.

8. How This Investigation Will Inform Your Own Music-Making

This final reflection connects Task A to later practical work.

Explain which ideas you may use in your own performance or composition.

You might discuss:

  • using a 12-bar blues progression
  • creating a riff
  • building a walking bass line
  • using call-and-response
  • improvising over a repeating structure
  • using blues scale or blue notes
  • creating expression through rhythm, articulation, or timbre

9. Sources

Include all sources you used.

Minimum expected sources:

  • 1 source about jazz/blues history
  • 1 source about the artist/composer/performer
  • 1 source about the specific composition or recording
  • 1 musical source: recording, score, lead sheet, transcription, or reliable analysis

Use credible sources where possible: books, museum/education sites, interviews, reputable music publications, documentaries, or high-quality analysis videos.

How This Is Marked (Criterion A: Investigating)

This task is marked out of 8.

  • 7-8 — Sophisticated: You investigate jazz/blues with strong detail and accuracy. Your critique of the example is specific, well-evidenced, and shows clear understanding of how the music communicates meaning. Sources are well chosen and properly used.
  • 5-6 — Proficient: You investigate jazz/blues with good detail. Your critique of the example is clear and includes musical evidence. You use music vocabulary correctly.
  • 3-4 — Developing: You cover the main sections of the task, but your investigation or critique is too short, unclear, or lacks specific evidence.
  • 1-2 — Beginning: Very little is complete. The investigation and critique are hard to understand.
  • 0 — Not submitted.

Differentiation

All students complete the same Criterion A goal: investigate jazz/blues and critique one approved musical example. You may use approved supports such as vocabulary lists, sentence starters, listening guides, teacher-approved sources, annotation guides, and staged checkpoints.

Differentiation is provided through content, process, product, language support, learning needs, and extension. The assessment criterion is not changed; access, scaffolding, complexity, and independence are adjusted according to your readiness and learning needs.

Support Options

  • teacher-approved example list
  • vocabulary bank for jazz/blues terms
  • 12-bar blues chord map
  • listening timeline template
  • sentence starters for analysis
  • model annotation of one short excerpt
  • source suggestions
  • teacher conference during planning

Extension Options

  • compare two performances of the same blues/jazz standard
  • analyse a more complex improvisation
  • include chord extensions or substitutions
  • transcribe a short riff or solo phrase
  • connect the example to later genre influence
  • explain how the same 12-bar structure can produce different expressive results

Academic Integrity

Your presenter notes must be written in your own words. You may use research sources, videos, articles, and AI tools only if allowed and properly acknowledged.

You must be able to explain your work in conversation. During or after the presentation, I may ask questions to verify your understanding.
