Unit Plan: Film Music

Back to Grade 10Open Blackhole

Description

Students investigate how film music shapes what audiences see, feel, and expect. They study film scoring history — from silent film accompaniment through classical Hollywood, leitmotif traditions, and modern hybrid scores — and the techniques composers use: leitmotif, timbre, harmony, synchronization, silence, and diegetic versus non-diegetic sound. After deconstructing a shared class example together, each student critiques one approved scene with an original composed score, explaining how the music changes visual perception. The unit prepares students for later practical work with music for visual narrative.

Inquiry Questions

Factual

  • What is a leitmotif, and what are diegetic and non-diegetic sound?
  • How did film scoring develop from silent film to modern hybrid scores?
  • What are hit points and synchronization?

Conceptual

  • How does music change the meaning of what we see?
  • How do composers guide an audience's emotional interpretation?
  • How do genre conventions shape what a score is allowed to do?

Debatable

  • Would the same scene mean something different with different music?
  • Is the best film music the music the audience doesn't notice?

Curriculum

Aims

  • Understand the relationship between art and its contexts
  • Develop the skills necessary to create and to perform art
  • Communicate through artistic expression

MYP Subject Group Objectives

  • Objective A: Investigating (Year 5 / Competent)
  • i: Investigate a movement, genre, or style in music, related to the statement of inquiry.
  • ii: Critique an artwork or performance from the chosen style.
  • Objective B: Developing
  • Practically explore ideas to inform development of a final artwork or performance.
  • Present a clear artistic intention for the final artwork or performance in line with the statement of inquiry.
  • Objective C: Creating/Performing
  • Create or perform an artwork.
  • Objective D: Evaluating
  • Appraise their own artwork or performance.
  • Reflect on their development as a musician.

Content

Factual Knowledge

  • Film music history: silent film, early synchronized sound, classical Hollywood, leitmotif and thematic scoring, electronic/hybrid and blockbuster traditions
  • Core techniques: leitmotif, timbre, melody/harmony, rhythm/tempo/ostinato, texture, dynamics, silence
  • Diegetic vs. non-diegetic sound; hit points and synchronization
  • Genre scoring conventions: horror, fantasy, action, romance, science fiction
  • Background of the chosen film, composer, and scene

Conceptual Understanding

  • Music does not accompany a scene; it changes what the scene means
  • The same visuals with different music produce different perceptions
  • Scores work through convention: audiences read musical codes they have learned
  • Critique requires evidence: timestamps, screenshots, and moment-by-moment analysis

Procedural Knowledge

  • Research film music history, the film, and the composer using credible sources
  • Deconstruct a scene: map visual action against musical action on a timeline
  • Identify techniques at specific timestamps and explain their perceptual effect
  • Build a presentation package: slides, full presenter notes, and a source list
  • Present live and answer follow-up questions

Skills

Subject-Specific Skills

  • Audio-visual analysis: hearing the score against the image
  • Film music vocabulary (leitmotif, timbre, diegetic, hit point)
  • Evidence-based critique of a specific scene
  • Connecting a score to scoring traditions and genre conventions

ATL Skills

  • Research (Information Literacy): evaluate and cite at least four credible sources across scoring history, film, composer, and the scene itself
  • Communication: build slides that support a spoken presentation; write full presenter notes
  • Critical Thinking: argue how the music changes perception, using the "how would it feel without this music?" test

IB Learner Profile

Attribute: Thinkers Students develop the Thinker attribute by separating what a scene shows from what its music makes the audience feel — a cause-and-effect analysis of perception. They reason from specific musical choices to specific audience effects and defend those claims with timestamped evidence.

International Mindedness

Film scoring is a global craft with strong cultural codes: the same emotion is scored differently across film traditions, and orchestral Hollywood conventions coexist with distinct national scoring styles. Students consider how musical codes are culturally learned, and how globalized film music spreads — and flattens — those differences.

Connections

Language Development

The unit requires sustained academic writing in presenter notes and precise film-music vocabulary, supported by a vocabulary bank and sentence starters. The live presentation develops spoken academic language; follow-up questions check genuine understanding beneath the prepared text.

Information Communication Technology

Students research online with source evaluation, work with video timestamps and screenshots, build scene timelines comparing visual and musical action, build slide decks, and submit a multi-file package (slides, Markdown or PDF notes) to the Blackhole.

Learning Experiences

In teaching order:

  1. Starter: watch the shared class scene (e.g. E.T.) with and without sound — what changes in what you feel and expect?
  2. Film scoring history lessons: silent film, early synchronized sound, classical Hollywood, leitmotif, electronic/hybrid and blockbuster scoring.
  3. Techniques lessons: leitmotif, timbre, diegetic vs. non-diegetic sound, hit points and synchronization, silence; short clips for each.
  4. Full class deconstruction of the model scene using the scene timeline template — visual action mapped against musical action.
  5. Scene choice and approval conferences (original composed score rule enforced); planning conference on each student's focus question.
  6. Research and independent deconstruction; draft scene timeline checked against the model.
  7. Build slides and presenter notes; pair rehearsal with structured feedback.
  8. Live presentations with teacher follow-up questions.

Assessment

Summative Tasks

  • Task A (Criterion A): presentation package — slides, mandatory presenter notes, source list — critiquing one approved film scene with an original composed score, presented live — see 10_TaskA_Film_Music.
  • Task BC (Criteria B, C): to be planned.
  • Task D (Criterion D): written reflection appraising their own work and their development as a musician.

Formative Assessment

  • Shared class deconstruction of a model scene (e.g. E.T., John Williams) before independent work
  • Scene choice approved by the teacher before analysis begins
  • Draft scene timeline compared against the model deconstruction
  • Planning conference on focus question and section coverage

Peer and Self-assessment

Students check their package against the nine required sections and the submission checklist, and rehearse presentations in pairs with structured feedback before presenting to the class.

Standardization and Moderation

As the sole music teacher, standardization relies on consistent application of MYP Arts Year 5 criteria, the marking bands in the Task A document, and holistic review of marks across classes; follow-up questioning verifies authenticity.

Feedback

Feedback is given at scene approval, during the shared class deconstruction, and at the draft-timeline checkpoint. Criterion-referenced comments target the key gap — "the music is scary so the scene is scary" description instead of perceptual critique — before final submission.

Differentiation

Advanced Students

  • Compare the scene with an alternative scoring possibility, or two scenes by the same composer
  • Include score/notation analysis if available
  • Trace a leitmotif across the wider film, or analyse music-sound design interaction

Struggling Students

  • Teacher-suggested scenes with clear, strong musical choices
  • Scene timeline template and model deconstruction as a close guide
  • Sentence starters for analysis

ELL Support

  • Film music vocabulary bank with plain-language definitions
  • Visual-heavy slides accepted; screenshots carry part of the argument
  • Presenter notes may use short, simple sentences

Resources

  • Model scene deconstruction, vocabulary bank, and scene timeline template (The Pantry)
  • Shared class example scene (e.g. E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial, John Williams)
  • Approved scene list / scene approval process
  • Credible sources: books, composer and director interviews, production notes, documentaries, reputable film music publications and analysis videos
