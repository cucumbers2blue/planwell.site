Task A: Film Music Investigation

Back to Grade 10Open Blackhole

Submission Tag: taskA

What This Task Is About

Film music changes how we understand a scene.

In this task, you will investigate one approved film scene. You will explain how the original composed score changes what the audience sees, feels, or expects.

We will first practise this together using a shared class example, such as a scene from E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial with music by John Williams. Then you will choose your own approved scene.

Statement Of Inquiry

Film composers use music to shape how audiences perceive visual meaning and narrative emotion.

What You Must Do

You must complete both parts.

  1. Investigate the context of film music, the film, and the composer.
  2. Critique one specific scene by explaining how the music changes visual perception.

This is because Criterion A has two strands:

  • Strand i: investigate a movement, genre, or style in music.
  • Strand ii: critique an artwork or performance from that style.

Where To Find Things

  • The model scene deconstruction is in The Pantry.
  • The Blackhole upload link is in The Pantry and/or this task page.
  • A film music vocabulary bank is in The Pantry.
  • A scene timeline template is in The Pantry.

Your Approved Scene

Your scene must:

  • come from a professionally produced film
  • use an original composed score by a credited composer
  • not rely mainly on a licensed pop song or pre-existing track
  • be about 2-5 minutes long
  • show clear musical choices
  • be school-appropriate
  • have enough detail for analysis

Possible scene types:

  • a character introduction
  • a chase, battle, or action scene
  • a moment of fear, wonder, sadness, or tension
  • a scene where music changes how the visuals feel
  • a scene with a clear theme, leitmotif, or orchestral cue

Your Presentation Package

Submit one presentation package:

  1. Presentation slides - visuals, screenshots, short keywords, and evidence
  2. Presenter notes - your full explanation in complete sentences
  3. Source list / bibliography - all sources you used

You will also present your work in class. I may ask follow-up questions to check your understanding.

Important: presenter notes are mandatory written evidence. Slides help the audience see and hear your ideas. Presenter notes show the depth of your investigation and analysis.

Required Presentation Sections

You do not need to use these exact slide numbers. But every section must appear somewhere in your presentation.

1. Approved Scene And Focus

Include:

  • film title, director, composer, and year
  • scene title or short description
  • exact clip length or timestamps
  • why this scene is suitable for analysis
  • your main investigation question

Example focus questions:

  • How does the music change the audience's perception of danger, wonder, fear, or sadness?
  • How does the score guide the viewer's emotional interpretation?
  • How would the scene feel different without this music?

2. Film Music History Context

Connect your scene to film music history.

Choose only the history that helps explain your scene. You may discuss:

  • silent film accompaniment
  • early synchronized sound
  • classical Hollywood orchestral scoring
  • leitmotif and thematic scoring
  • electronic or hybrid scoring
  • blockbuster scoring traditions
  • genre conventions such as horror, fantasy, action, romance, or science fiction

3. Film And Composer Background

Investigate the film and composer.

Include:

  • film background and genre
  • composer background and style
  • production context, if it matters
  • what sources say about the score or composer's approach
  • how the scene fits into the larger film

4. Key Film Music Techniques

Explain the techniques that matter in your scene.

Possible features:

  • leitmotif = a theme linked to a character, place, or idea
  • timbre = the sound colour of instruments or voices
  • melody and harmony
  • rhythm, tempo, pulse, or ostinato
  • texture and dynamics
  • silence
  • diegetic and non-diegetic sound
  • hit points and synchronization
  • sound design and mixing choices
  • form and structure over time

Use accurate music vocabulary. Define terms when needed.

5. Detailed Scene Deconstruction

Analyse the scene closely.

Include specific evidence such as:

  • timestamps
  • screenshots
  • clip or audio examples
  • a scene timeline
  • score or music sheet excerpts, if available
  • annotated moments
  • visual action compared with musical action

Explain how the music works, not only what happens.

Useful questions:

  • What does the viewer see?
  • What does the viewer hear?
  • What changes when the music enters, changes, or stops?
  • How do instruments, rhythm, harmony, texture, dynamics, or silence shape perception?
  • What moments are synchronized with visual action?
  • What meaning does the music add or change?

6. How The Music Changes Visual Perception

Critique how the music affects the audience's interpretation.

Go beyond description.

Weak: "The music is scary and makes the scene scary."

Stronger: "The low sustained strings and unresolved harmony make the empty hallway feel threatening before anything dangerous appears on screen. The music changes the viewer's perception from neutral space to possible danger."

Consider:

  • What does the music make the audience notice?
  • What emotion or expectation does the music create?
  • Does the music support, contradict, or complicate the visuals?
  • How might the same scene feel with different music?
  • What genre or cultural conventions does the music use?

7. Connection To The Statement Of Inquiry

Answer this directly:

How does this scene show that film composers use music to shape visual meaning and narrative emotion?

Use evidence from your research and scene deconstruction.

8. How This Investigation Will Inform Your Own Music-Making

Explain which ideas you may use later if you compose, select, adapt, or design music for a visual scene.

You might discuss:

  • using timbre to change mood
  • using tempo or rhythm to control pacing
  • using harmony to create tension or release
  • using leitmotif to represent a character or idea
  • using silence for dramatic effect
  • synchronizing music with visual action
  • changing audience perception through musical contrast

9. Sources

Include all sources used.

Minimum expected sources:

  • 1 source about film music history or film scoring
  • 1 source about the film or production context
  • 1 source about the composer or score
  • 1 musical/film source, such as the scene itself, soundtrack recording, score excerpt, interview, or reliable analysis

Use credible sources where possible. Good sources include books, composer interviews, director interviews, production notes, documentaries, reputable film music publications, or high-quality analysis videos.

How This Is Marked (Criterion A: Investigating)

This task is marked out of 8.

  • 7-8 - Sophisticated: You investigate film music with strong detail and accuracy. Your scene critique is specific, well-evidenced, and explains how music changes visual perception. Sources are well chosen and properly used.
  • 5-6 - Proficient: You investigate film music with good detail. Your scene critique is clear and includes musical evidence. You use music and film-music vocabulary correctly.
  • 3-4 - Developing: You cover the main sections, but the investigation or critique is too short, general, unclear, or missing specific evidence.
  • 1-2 - Beginning: Very little is complete. The investigation and scene critique are hard to understand.
  • 0 - Not submitted.

Differentiation

All students complete the same Criterion A goal: investigate film music and critique one approved film scene.

You may use approved supports such as vocabulary lists, sentence starters, listening guides, teacher-approved sources, scene timeline templates, annotation guides, and staged checkpoints.

Differentiation changes the support, complexity, and independence. It does not change the Criterion A standard.

Support Options

  • teacher-approved scene suggestions
  • film music vocabulary bank
  • scene timeline template
  • model deconstruction from the shared class example
  • sentence starters for analysis
  • source suggestions
  • teacher conference during scene approval or planning

Extension Options

  • compare the scene with an alternative scoring possibility
  • compare two scenes by the same composer
  • include score or notation analysis if available
  • investigate leitmotif across the wider film
  • analyse how sound design and music interact
  • connect the score to a wider film music tradition

Academic Integrity

Your presenter notes must be written in your own words. You may use research sources, videos, articles, and AI tools only if allowed by the teacher and properly acknowledged.

You must be able to explain your work in conversation. During or after the presentation, I may ask questions to verify your understanding.
